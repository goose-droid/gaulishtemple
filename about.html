Ephemeral Written Prayer

As a polytheist, I'm often saying small prayers of praise or petition to the gods. However, I prefer writing them since I find it easier to formulate my words when I am putting those words to paper. Because of that, Iâ€™m often writing prayers out on notebook pages, typing them into the margins of school papers on LibreOffice, and scribbling them down on the back of receipts from the grocery store. These prayers are still not meant to last beyond the immediate moment. The notebook pages get forgotten, the margin-prayers in my essay get deleted before I turn the paper in, and the receipts get tossed in the recycling. In these cases, the action of writing is more important than the result.

This site is meant as a place to write down these prayers, when a pencil and notebook may not be as practical as a phone that can be held in one hand, and when you want to pray in a space slightly nicer than the margins of a math notebook, or in a Notepad window. Digital Offerings

Before romanization, the Gauls rarely wrote. And no evidence, historical or archaeological, can tell us the specific words they spoke aloud in prayer. One thing that archaeology has provided us in plenty, however, is the remains of the various non-food offerings the ancient Celts made to the gods.

Archaeologists have found weapons buried in the ground or deposited into bodies of water such as lakes and rivers. Some of these weapons were offered broken or bent, thereby symbolically ending their "life" and devoting them to the gods instead of to human use. Later on, we find the dedication and deposit of various non-weapon votive offerings, including money, pottery, and statuettes more common. Of the latter, there are many models of human body parts, likely offered in hopes of healing an injury or sickness (See: The Celts, ed. by Venceslas Kruta et al., and Les Religions Gauloises, by Jean-Louis Brunaux).

Today, we can offer not only these physical items, but ones made of 1s and 0s, like digital art. But what is the digital equivalent of placing an object on an altar? There are many possible answers to this. Many people have created their own, unique digital altars, and many others may not even feel the need for a digital altar space. Iâ€™ve found a solution that works for me, which is the simple file attachment on the home page. Choosing a file then clearing it alongside a short prayer is a short ritualistic action that makes the dedication of some bit of digital creation feel complete.

Future Plans for the Site

In the future, I hope to implement:

  • A candle that can be "lit" and "put out"
  • More background images
  • More thorough background information about offerings and prayer within Gaulish, general Celtic, and general Polytheist contexts, both ancient and modern.
  • Continual small improvements as I improve my html, css, and javascript skills

In addition to the above, someday I may try to implement a secondary page where visitors can submit prayers to keep and display for others, creating a sort of online prayer book.
